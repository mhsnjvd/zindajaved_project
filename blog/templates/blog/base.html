One more toss

April 16, 2017

Suppose Asma and Babar decide to play a coin tossing game. We suppose that Asma tosses \(n\) fair coins while Babar tosses \(n+1\) fair coins. The player with more heads wins the game. What is the probability that Babar wins the game?

Let us define the following random variables.

$$A_n = \text{# of heads thrown by Asma in $n$ tosses},$$ $$B_n = \text{# of heads thrown by Babar in $n$ tosses}.$$
We are trying to find the probability \(P(B_{n+1} > A_n)\). Let us now condition on the first \(n\) tosses. If \(B_{n+1} > A_n\), then either \(B_n = A_n\), which means that the final toss by Babar decides the game, or \(B_n > A_n\), in which case Babar has already won the game in the first \(n\) tosses. Mathematically, we can write this using the law of total probability $$P(B_{n+1} > A_n) = P(B_{n+1} > A_n | B_n > A_n) P(B_n > A_n)+ P(B_{n+1} > A_n | B_n = A_n)P(B_n = A_n).$$

We have $$P(B_{n+1} > A_n | B_n > A_n) = 1.$$ However, $$P(B_{n+1} > A_n | B_n=A_n ) = 1/2,$$ since Babar's \(n+1)^{st}\) toss is a head only with probability \(1/2\).

We can therefore write $$P(B_{n+1} > A_n) = 1 \times P(B_n > A_n) + \frac{1}{2} P(B_n = A_n).$$ We also have the identity $$P(A_n = B_n) + P(A_n > B_n) + P(A_n < B_n) = 1.$$ Let \(P(A_n = B_n) = x\) and \(P(A_n > B_n) = p\). By symmetry, we also have \(P(B_n > A_n) = p\). Therefore, $$2p + x = 1,$$ which implies $$p = \frac{1 - x}{2}.$$ Using this expression, we have $$P(B_{n+1} > A_n) = \frac{1 - x}{2} + \frac{1}{2} x,$$ which simplifies to the surprising result $$P(B_{n+1} > A_n) = \frac{1}{2}.$$

What if \(x^*Ax\) is real?

April 3, 2019

Let \(A\) be a square matrix in \(\mathbb{C}^{n \times n} \), and suppose that for any vector \(x\) in \(\mathbb{C}^n\), \(x^*Ax \) is a real number. What can we conclude about \(A\)?

As we shall prove below, we can actually show that \(A\) is Hermitian, i.e. \(A = A^*\).

Let \( x, y \) be vectors in \(\mathbb{C}^n\), then the number \( (x + y)^*A(x+y)\) is real by assumption and therefore equals its conjugate, i.e., $$ (x + y)^*A(x+y) = (x + y)^*A^*(x+y), $$ Which after expansion and cancellation of some terms gives, $$ x^*Ay + y^*Ax = x^*A^*y + y^*A^*x. $$ Let \(x\) and \(y\) be unit vectors along the i-th and j-th axis respectively, i.e., let \(x = e_i, y = e_j\) in the above equation and we get, $$ a_{ij} + a_{ji} = \overline{a_{ji}} + \overline{a_{ij}}, $$ which implies $$ a_{ij} + a_{ji} = \overline{a_{ij} + a_{ji}}, $$ i.e., the number \(a_{ij} + a_{ji} \) equals its conjugate and is therefore real. This implies, $$ Im(a_{ij}) = -Im(a_{ji}) $$ Similarly, now choose \(x = ie_i, y=e_j\) and we get $$ -ia_{ij} + ia_{ji} = -i\overline{a_{ji}} + i\overline{a_{ij}}, $$ which after cancelling the imaginary unit \(i\), gives us $$ -a_{ij} + a_{ji} = -\overline{a_{ji}} + \overline{a_{ij}}, $$ which we can further re-write as, $$ a_{ij} - a_{ji} = -\overline{\left(a_{ij} - a_{ji}\right)}, $$ $$ a_{ij} - a_{ji} +\overline{\left(a_{ij} - a_{ji}\right)} = 0, $$ $$ 2 Re(a_{ij} - a_{ji}) = 0. $$ This implies, $$ Re(a_{ij}) = Re(a_{ji}). $$ The above, together with the earlier equation involing imaginary parts gives us $$ a_{ij} = \overline{a_{ji}}. $$ Therefore, $$ A = A^*. $$ Let us first write \(A\) as $$ A = B + iC, $$ where $$ B = \frac{1}{2} \left(A+A^*\right), $$ and $$ C = \frac{1}{2i} \left(A-A^*\right). $$ Note that both \(B\) and \(C\) are Hermitian.
